> Great question. Yes — Julius Plücker. He's the closest structural counterpart.
>
> Plücker was the champion of analytic geometry in roughly the same period and milieu. And the rivalry was _personal_, not just intellectual — much like Brouwer-Hilbert. Steiner reportedly used his influence with Crelle (the journal editor) to try to marginalize Plücker's work and make it harder for him to publish. Plücker was sufficiently discouraged that he actually left mathematics for a stretch and went into physics (he did important work on spectral lines and cathode rays), before eventually returning.
>
> So you get the same structure: two figures who don't just disagree about methods but whose disagreement becomes institutionally and personally bitter, with real consequences for careers and publication.
>
> One difference worth noting — and I'm curious whether you think this breaks the structural parallel or not — is the power asymmetry. In the Brouwer-Hilbert case, it was ultimately _Hilbert_ (the formalist) who had the institutional upper hand and effectively won the sociological battle. In the Steiner-Plücker case, it was _Steiner_ (the purist/intuitionist-analog) who wielded the institutional power, at least for a time. The "winner" in terms of the long arc of mathematics flipped too — analytic methods thoroughly won out in geometry.
